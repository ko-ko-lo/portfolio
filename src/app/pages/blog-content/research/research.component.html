Quantitative Research Briefly Explained

Quantitative Research revolves around data. It includes things like Analytics, A/B Testing, and Key Performance Metrics. What makes Quantitative Research valuable is that it's comparable, measurable, and doesn't require direct interaction with users.

This type of research helps teams make better product decisions by revealing whether those decisions are effective or not.

Qualitative Research Briefly Explained

Qualitative Research, on the other hand, is all about understanding people. It involves speaking with users or observing their behavior. This method is excellent for building empathy and gaining a deeper understanding of the individuals for whom the product is being developed.

Examples of Qualitative Research include Usability Testing or Observational Studies where you watch how people use a product in a natural environment.

  • Quantitative Research asks: What is happening?
  • Qualitative Research asks: Why is it happening?

Practical Example: Improving User Engagement in a Yoga App

Let's imagine we have a yoga app that provides users with on-demand yoga classes. Recently, we noticed that many users are not completing their first session after signing up. To improve the user experience and boost engagement, we want to understand where users are dropping off during their initial yoga session.

Through Quantitative Research, we learn that only 40% of new users who start a session actually finish it. The largest drop-off happens around the 10-minute mark of a 30-minute beginner class.

Quantitative Research gives us this insight, showing us exactly when users are disengaging. Now, we can focus on this specific moment in the session to find out why users are leaving early.

Using Qualitative Research to Understand Why

To dig deeper, we conduct Qualitative Research in the form of User Interviews and Observational Studies. By talking with users and observing their behavior, we aim to understand why they drop off at this point.

Observed Problem

In our Observational Study, we find that new users feel overwhelmed or physically fatigued around the 10-minute mark. The pace of the beginner class, while manageable for regular users, seems too fast for true beginners. Some users also mention that they're unsure how to modify poses to suit their level.

Design Decision for a Possible Solution

Based on this qualitative feedback, we make a design decision to add short breaks at the 10-minute mark in beginner classes, providing users with an option to pause, hydrate, or adjust their posture. We also introduce on-screen tips for pose modifications and include more encouraging language during this critical phase of the session to reassure users they can go at their own pace.

Measuring the Impact with Quantitative Research

After implementing these changes, we return to Quantitative Research to measure the impact. We look at whether the number of users completing the session increases and analyze if there are fewer drop-offs around the 10-minute mark. This approach helps us determine if our solution is successful in improving user engagement.

Conclusion: The Power of Combining Quantitative and Qualitative Research

Both Qualitative and Quantitative Research methods are powerful tools for improving the user experience. Quantitative Research focuses on data, while Qualitative Research listens to and observes users.

When combined, these two approaches provide a more comprehensive understanding of the product and its users, allowing you to develop thoughtful and effective solutions. By using both methods together, you can generate valuable insights and create a better product experience.
