Here's how I approach design projects today.

1. Research & Discovery: Asking the Right Questions

This is where everything begins. It's the part where I slow down, listen, and observe before rushing into ideas or visuals.

Some key questions I try to answer:

  • Who are we designing for?
  • What are the needs, desires, and goals of both users and the business?
  • Where and how is the product used?
  • What will make the experience engaging and relevant?

This phase is about understanding the context: getting to know the company's vision, interviewing users, stakeholders, and immersing myself in the ecosystem. Sometimes I'm handed personas and user journeys, other times, I need to help create them.

The activities in this phase can include:

  • Stakeholder interviews
  • User interviews and observations
  • Competitor analysis
  • Moodboards or visual research
  • Workshops
  • Personas, user journeys, or experience maps

The outcome? A shared understanding of the problem space and an alignment between vision, goals, and user needs.

2. High-Level Conceptual Design: Structuring the Experience

This is where the product starts to take shape, not in pixels, but in ideas, flows, and structures. Based on everything we learned in the research phase, we start outlining how the product will function and feel.

Some Key questions:

  • How should the information be structured?
  • What are the key tasks users need to accomplish, and how can we make those tasks intuitive?
  • What interactions will support those tasks effectively?
  • What should the user journey look like?

Typical outcomes:

  • Information architecture (e.g., sitemap, navigation)
  • User and task flows
  • Low-fidelity wireframes or sketches
  • Interaction principles or design guidelines
  • Basic clickable prototypes

This phase sets the foundation. It gives everyone (designers, developers, stakeholders) a shared blueprint of what we're building.

3. Detailed Design: Bringing the Product to Life

Now comes the part where things get real. This phase is about turning structure into experience. It is bringing personality, polish, and interactivity to the product.

Some of the things I focus on here:

  • Creating or using a design system
  • Finalizing the visual language (color, typography, grid, spacing)
  • Designing each screen in detail
  • Prototyping the actual interactions
  • Ensuring accessibility and usability
  • Clarifying UI behavior and microinteractions

Here, system thinking becomes essential. I often work closely with developers to ensure designs are feasible and consistent. The outcome is a ready-for-development design which is polished, documented, and grounded in all the insights we've gathered.

4. Testing & Iteration: Learning and Improving

Design isn't done when the screens are handed off. In fact, it's never really done. People use products in unexpected ways, and assumptions, even well-researched ones, don't always hold.

That's why I believe testing and iteration should happen throughout:

  • Early: with wireframes or prototypes
  • During: usability tests as the design develops
  • After: evaluating the live product and improving

Testing helps validate (or challenge) our decisions. It's where we learn what works, what doesn't, and how we can improve. It also keeps us honest by reminding us that design is ultimately for users, not just stakeholders or teams.

In Closing: Why This Process Works for Me

Every designer eventually finds their rhythm. This process isn't revolutionary, but it's mine. It helps me stay focused while being flexible, user-centered while pragmatic. I can adapt it to messy startups or enterprise clients, short sprints or long-term projects.
