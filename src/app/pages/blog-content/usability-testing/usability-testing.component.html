1. Preparation of a Test Plan

1.1 General Information

The test plan begins with basic details about the Usability Test: the product being tested, the focus areas, the responsible UX Experts, and any other relevant information, such as the time allocated per task.

1.2 Description of an Initial Scenario

The initial scenario sets the stage, helping the test user visualize a real-life situation. For instance:

You are a sales representative in the insurance industry. You are meeting a client who is interested in life insurance. You've arranged this meeting to present them with an offer. You are using new software that helps you create an accurate risk analysis.

1.3 Writing Test Objectives and Test Items

The core of the test plan revolves around the test objectives and test items, which are created based on the goals of the test.

Example of a Test Objective:

Test how smoothly users are guided through a form's input fields to quickly generate an accurate life insurance quote.

Example of a Test Task:

Your client provides you with the necessary details for a life insurance policy. Please enter all the information into the form. Stop when you reach the field for the policy term.

It's helpful to start with simpler tasks to build the test user's confidence.

1.4 Play Through the Test Yourself

Before conducting the Usability Test, ensure that the tasks are achievable by playing through them yourself. A test run helps confirm that the product supports the tasks you've created.

Tips for Creating a Test Plan:

  • Provide a clear description of the test objectives and their expected outcomes.
  • Make sure all necessary information for the test is included.
  • Set a time limit for task completion (but keep it hidden from the user).
  • Use clear, simple language for all test tasks.

2. Conducting the Usability Test

Now it's time to see how easily users can complete the tasks.

2.1 Check the Test Environment

Before the participants arrive, double-check that all technical equipment, such as laptops or other devices, is functioning perfectly.

2.2 Welcoming and Instructing Participants

Greet the participants warmly and explain the process. It's helpful to reassure them that it's the software being tested, not them. This helps reduce any potential anxiety and makes them feel more comfortable.

2.3 Observing Test Users

As participants navigate the product, observe and collect feedback based on their interactions. Feedback can be recorded via video (with consent), screen recordings, or written notes.

2.4 Final Interview

After the tasks are completed, an optional interview allows you to gather further insights into participants' opinions and impressions of the product. You can also ask them to evaluate the design and overall experience.

I like to ask participants to complete a (digital) questionnaire at the end for additional feedback.

Tips for Conducting Usability Tests:

  • Encourage participants to think aloud as they complete the tasks.
  • Avoid interrupting unless absolutely necessary.
  • Prompt participants to work through challenges rather than offering solutions right away.
  • Offer refreshments when welcoming participants.

3. Analyzing the Test Data

3.1 Categorizing Usability Problems

Usability testing aims to uncover challenges users face when completing tasks. To quantify these issues, I categorize them into three types:

  • Minor Problems: Users hesitate briefly or show mild confusion.
  • Moderate Problems: Users experience confusion or make a self-correcting error.
  • Severe Problems: Users are unable to complete a task or make multiple errors.

3.2 Prioritizing Usability Issues

Once problems are categorized, I prioritize them based on urgency and how many users encountered the issue.

Other important metrics may include:

Quantitative Metrics:

  • Success rate for tasks
  • Time to complete tasks
  • Number of errors or clicks

Qualitative Metrics:

  • Stress reactions
  • Subjective satisfaction
  • Perceived effort
  • Joy of use

4. Writing a Test Report

One of the most critical parts of Usability Testing is sharing the results in a clear and persuasive way.

4.1 Introduction

Begin the report with a brief summary of the Usability Test. Outline the goals, focus areas, and key findings. I also like to start on a positive note by highlighting strengths, such as: “During the Usability Test, the intuitive layout of the tool received high praise.”

Next, describe the methodology, for example:

  • Participants: 5 (4 female, 1 non-binary), Age: 30 - 45
  • Laboratory Testing
  • 1:1 sessions
  • Tasks completed independently
  • Followed by a post-test questionnaire

4.2 Main Section

The main body of the report lists usability problems for each task, often supported by screenshots where appropriate. Alongside each issue, mention the task success rate and the number of errors found. Include actionable recommendations to improve the product.

Usability problems should be prioritized to help the team focus on the most critical issues first.

4.3 Final Section

The final section gathers the opinions and reflections of the test users. Both positive and negative comments can be shared here (ideally quoted verbatim). If a questionnaire was used, summarize the results in this section.

Tips for Creating a Test Report:

  • Focus on the outcomes that matter most.
  • Keep the company's goals in mind.
  • Be mindful of any technological limitations.
  • Clearly differentiate between personal opinions and established facts.

Conclusion on Usability Testing

Usability Testing uncovers the needs and challenges users experience while interacting with a product. It provides data-driven insights, allowing design decisions to be based on facts, not assumptions.

Not every aspect of a product can be tested, so it's wise to focus on critical or problematic areas. As observers during Usability Testing, we must resist the temptation to influence users.

Prioritizing findings is crucial, taking into account the organization's goals and technical limitations. Lastly, communicating test results effectively is key to gaining stakeholder support for improvements.
